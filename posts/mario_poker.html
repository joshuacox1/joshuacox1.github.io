Solving Mario Poker

Picture Poker is a playable minigame in New Super Mario Bros. on the Nintendo DS. It is a simple variant of five-card draw poker.

The deck consists of 30 cards, 5 each of the following (increasing in value from left to right):

Luigi deals 5 cards to you and himself. You can choose to wager some number of coins, then throw any subset of your cards away and be dealt replacements, like in normal five-card draw. Luigi then throws some of his cards (he always throws his singletons) and is dealt replacements. Then there is a showdown.

The hand ranks are shown above. There are no flushes or straights. Also, unlike in normal poker, only pairs or higher are considered as tie-breakers.

If you win against Luigi, you receive the listed number of coins as a payout (though one of them is the coin you wagered, which is a bit of a scam). If you draw, you receive your wagered coin back. If you lose, you lose your coin. You also have the opportunity to wager up to five coins as your bankroll grows, which just multiplies all the above numbers by the wagered amount.

And grow your bankroll will. If you’ve been paying attention, you will notice this bet structure is completely ridiculous and Luigi is possibly the worst casino operator of all time. He is literally haemorrhaging money to whoever turns up.

There are many questions we might have about Picture Poker. What’s the optimal playing strategy? Does it differ depending on whether you maximise expected coins or maximise chance of winning? How likely is each hand with the best strategy? More on this below, but it turns out that optimal strategy can be described in one sentence:

Keep your highest card with Junk, throw away singletons with everything else, and always wager the maximum number of coins.

This is an interesting result and it means that the only time Luigi plays less than perfectly is when he’s dealt Junk (he bins the lot). It is unsurprisingly not trivial and requires calculation. Luckily Picture Poker is a small enough game that it can be brute-forced.

Brute-forcing Picture Poker

I’m not going to talk much about how you would brute-force Picture Poker, but there are two convenient structures that come up. One is to recognise that everything is just a multiset and implement things in terms of a multiset algebra. The deck is always a multiset, your hand is a multiset, you throw away some multisubset of cards from your hand, and so on. This unifies everything, it’s easy to implement without mutation and your life will be a lot easier. Another is to avoid duplication during the generation of size-\(n\) multisubsets of a set by observing that the probability of selecting \(n\) cards from a multiset \(X=\{d_1^{(x_1)},\ldots,d_m^{(x_m)}\}\) with \(n\leq |X|\) and obtaining \(Y=\{d_1^{(y_1)},\ldots,d_m^{(y_m)}\}\) is

\[ \left[\prod_{i=1}^m \binom{y_i}{x_i} \right]\bigg/ \binom{|Y|}{|X|} \]

and thus just returning pairs of multisubsets and their associated probability.

Anyway, having done this, what does the data say? It turns out that for every possible starting hand, there is a unique play that is best under both maximising £££ and maximising the chance of winning. More surprisingly, it turns out that every starting hand has positive expected value of coinage with the correct play. Even the worst possible starting hand

yields \(\frac{3818310866}{58561878375} \approx 0.060251\) coins in expectation with the optimal play of keeping the Mario and dumping the rest. (Keeping just the Luigi is the only other play that scrapes in just above zero.) This is despite you losing to Luigi about two-thirds of the time. His bet structure is just that absurdly advantageous to you.

With optimal play (in either sense of maximising E[coins] or maximising win %), we have overall outcomes per hand as follows:

  • Expected coins: \(\frac{3525093602272}{3034697832621} \approx 1.161596\)
  • Win: \(\frac{16824472597853}{33381676158831} \approx 50.4003\%\)
  • Draw: \(\frac{1311546800}{112396216023} \approx 1.1669\%\)
  • Loss: \(\frac{16167674161378}{33381676158831} \approx 48.4328\%\)

And the distribution of hand types under optimal play:

  • Five of a Kind: \(\frac{1321}{1567566} \approx 0.0843\%\)
  • Four of a Kind: \(\frac{1343435}{36054018} \approx 3.7262\%\)
  • Full House: \(\frac{67150}{546273} \approx 12.2924\%\)
  • Three of a Kind: \(\frac{4001950}{18027009} \approx 22.1997\%\)
  • Two Pair: \(\frac{14300}{42021} \approx 34.0306\%\)
  • One Pair: \(\frac{120500}{462231} \approx 26.0692\%\)
  • Junk: \(\frac{32000}{2003001} \approx 1.5976\%\)

(Note that the above is not necessarily the play that maximises your chance of any given hand type or higher. If you have a full house, the optimal play in terms of both E[coins] and win% is to keep your whole hand. But if you wanted to maximise your chance of getting five of a kind, you would discard the pair and pray.)

Mapping English words to sequences of the 44 phonemes



Dictionary phonetics typically analyse British RP English as consisting of 44 phonemes, in the main: 24 consonants and 20 vowels (12 single, 8 double). Words are then transcribed as sequences of these phonemes, with a stress mark attached. Some compl

  • The short vowels in happ*y* and us*u*al, sometimes transcribed /i/ and /u/ respectively, are written identically /iː/ as /uː/.
  • Rare sounds occurring only in loanwords, such as Scottish /x/, Welsh /ɬ/, and the French nasal vowels /ã/ and /ɒ̃/, are not mapped.
  • The so-called “vowels in free variation” such as in hon*e*st and J*u*ly, sometimes transcribed /ᵻ/ and /ᵿ/ respectively, are written as /ɪ/ and /ʊ/.
  • The syllabic consonants (l, m, n) are notated as /əl/, /əm/, /ən/. (TODO: NOT SURE!)
  • Common words with strong and weak pronunciations are written *as spoken*. The two pronunciations of "the" depending on whether the next word starts with a consonant or a vowel are also written as spoken. The pronunciation of "have" in "have to" as "haf" is also respected. (Note that the former makes machine transcription non-trivial.)
  • Unaffected punctuation.

Mapping phoneme sequences to Tengwar

  • The basic mapping from phonemes to Tengwar glyphs is given in the table.
  • If a short vowel (one of the seven single vowels with a short carrier ) is followed by a consonant, the vowel’s diacritic is written over that consonant instead: for example, “patch” is not  but .
    • The “schwa” vowel is written with the  as a diacritic, but as simply  alone. For example, “alpha” is , but “altered” is .
  • The  and  forms of S and Z are used with no tehtar, or with a single dot above or below. With more complex tehtar or an overline, the nuquerna forms  and  are used instead. For example, “sizzle” is , but “jazz” is . This is not withstanding the flourish rule below:
  • Unstressed -s/-z endings are indicated with a flourish diacritic () rather than a written-out  or . In particular:
    • -ps, -fs, -ts, -ths, -cs (e.g. “baths” )
    • -bz, -vz, -dz, -thz, -gz, -mz, -nz, -ngz (e.g. “sings” )
    • -siz, -shiz, -chiz, -ziz, -zhiz, -jiz (e.g. “” )
    • any vowel + z (e.g. “woes” )

  • Certain nasal consonants are reduced to an overbar diacritic when they are succeeded by a consonant. For example, “lamp” is not  but ; “injure” is not  but . The nasal in particular is as follows:
    •  before , 
    •  before , , , , , , , , 
    •  before , .
    The rule does not apply to separate words recently joined together.
  • The letter  is used when “r” is used alone as a sound. When “r” is used in consonant combinations,  is used. For example, “carrot” ; “price” ; but note “shipwreck” .

    • “the” – 
    • “and the” – 
    • “of” – 
    • “of the” – 

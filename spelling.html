The idea is to map phonemic British RP pronunciations from a reasonable dictionary, ignoring stress and aspiration, to a more natural set of characters.

Lastly:

  • The word “the” is written ḍə before consonants and ḍí before vowels, reflecting the pronunciation. Other words like “to” and “into” are treated similarly.
  • Existing spelling conventions such as capitalised “I”, possessive “’s”, and apostrophes in contractions such as “I’ve” and “she’d” are ignored. Rules around capitalisation and other punctuation (including hyphens) remain unchanged.

Examples

The North Wind and the Sun

The North Wind and the Sun were disputing which was the stronger, when a traveler came along wrapped in a warm cloak. They agreed that the one who first succeeded in making the traveler take his cloak off should be considered stronger than the other. Then the North Wind blew as hard as he could, but the more he blew the more closely did the traveler fold his cloak around him; and at last the North Wind gave up the attempt. Then the Sun shone out warmly, and immediately the traveler took off his cloak. And so the North Wind was obliged to confess that the Sun was the stronger of the two.

Ḍə Nooṭ Wind ənd ḍə Săn

Ḍə Nooṭ Wind and ḍə Săn wə dispyuutiṇ wić wəz ḍə stroṇgə, wen ə travələ ceim əloṇ rapt in ə woom cləuc. Ḍei əgriid ḍət ḍə wăn huu fəəst săcsiidid in meiciṇ ḍə travələ teic hiz cləuc of shud bii cənsidəd stroṇgə ḍən ḍí ăḍə. Ḍen ḍə Nooṭ Wind bluu əz haad əz hii cud, băt ḍə moo hii bluu ḍə moo cləuslí did ḍə travələ fəuld hiz cləuc əaund him; and ət laast ḍə Nooṭ Wind geiv ăp ḍí ətempt. Ḍen ḍə Săn śon aut woomlí, ənd imiidiətlí ḍə travələ tuc of hiz cləuc. Ənd səu ḍə Nooṭ Wind wəz əblaijd tə cənfes ḍət ḍə Săn wəz ḍə stroṇgə əv ḍə tuu.

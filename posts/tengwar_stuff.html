Mapping English words to sequences of the 44 phonemes



Dictionary phonetics typically analyse British RP English as consisting of 44 phonemes, in the main: 24 consonants and 20 vowels (12 single, 8 double). Words are then transcribed as sequences of these phonemes, with a stress mark attached. Some compl

  • The short vowels in happ*y* and us*u*al, sometimes transcribed /i/ and /u/ respectively, are written identically /iː/ as /uː/.
  • Rare sounds occurring only in loanwords, such as Scottish /x/, Welsh /ɬ/, and the French nasal vowels /ã/ and /ɒ̃/, are not mapped.
  • The so-called “vowels in free variation” such as in hon*e*st and J*u*ly, sometimes transcribed /ᵻ/ and /ᵿ/ respectively, are written as /ɪ/ and /ʊ/.
  • The syllabic consonants (l, m, n) are notated as /əl/, /əm/, /ən/. (TODO: NOT SURE!)
  • Common words with strong and weak pronunciations are written *as spoken*. The two pronunciations of "the" depending on whether the next word starts with a consonant or a vowel are also written as spoken. The pronunciation of "have" in "have to" as "haf" is also respected. (Note that the former makes machine transcription non-trivial.)
  • Unaffected punctuation.

Mapping phoneme sequences to Tengwar

  • The basic mapping from phonemes to Tengwar glyphs is given in the table.
  • Single vowels before a consonant phoneme are represented as their diacritic over the glyph for that consonant. Before another vowel or as the final phoneme of a word, they are written as presented.
  • The primary forms of the glyphs for S and Z (silme and essë) are used when the letter has no diacritic and is not the final letter of a word. Otherwise the inverted forms (silme nuquerna, essë nuquerna) are used. The word "is" is an exception to this rule: it is written with ordinary essë with a dot to the left.
  • If not decided earlier: syllabic consonants have no written vowel.

Complexities

  • //
  • If a single vowel is followed by a consonant, the vowel’s diacritic is written over that consonant instead: for example, “patch” is not  but .
    • The “schwa” vowel, which doesn't have a diacritic
  •  and  are used when no diacritics are present and the letter is not the final letter of the word. When there are diacritics or the letter occurs word-finally (and isn't covered by the flourish rule below),  and  are used instead. (The sole exception to this rule is the word “is”, which is often written .)
  • Unstressed -s/-z endings are indicated with a flourish diacritic () rather than a written-out  and . In particular:
    • -ps, -fs, -ts, -ths, -cs
    • -bz, -vz, -dz, -thz, -gz, -mz, -wz, -nz, -ngz
    • -siz, -shiz, -chiz, -ziz, -zhiz, -jiz
    (Font support here is spotty. In the font Alcarin used here, the ára  must be shortened to the telco  for this diacritic to look reasonable ()).
  • Certain common words or phrases have their own shorthand forms:
    • “and” – is normally  but can be simply 
    • “the” – 
    • “and the” –  or 
    • “of” – 
    • “have” – , “has” – 
    • “of the” – 
  • Nasalised consonants are reduced to an overbar diacritic. For example, “lamp” is not  but ; “lamp” is not  but . The nasal in particular is as follows:
